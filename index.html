Assumptions (fixed)
  • Property & Tax
  • Property tax: CA Prop 13: 1.25% of purchase price, +2%/yr max
  • Insurance: 0.5% of current property value/yr
  • Maintenance: 1% of current property value/yr
  • Depreciation (rental only): IRS 27.5-yr schedule; improvement % adjustable above. Primary residences are not eligible for depreciation deductions.
  • Tax rate: 44% (35% fed + 9.3% CA) on paper rental gain/loss
  • Capital & Returns
  • Initial capital: $100K for all scenarios
  • Wealth = Current Equity + Cumulative Net Cash Flows — equity is home value minus remaining mortgage balance; cash flows are total rent collected minus all expenses paid out of pocket
  • Reinvested mode: positive cash flows compound at S&P 500 monthly returns; deficit periods accumulate additively (treated as out-of-pocket cost, not leveraged against the market)
  • Note: for highly leveraged scenarios (e.g. 25% down with high mortgage rates), net cash flows are often negative throughout — Reinvested and Additive modes will produce identical results since there is no positive pool to compound
  • Additive mode: dividends & rent cash flows accumulate without compounding
  • Refinance
  • Refi count (0–3): number of refinances during the holding period
  • Purchase rate is the 30-yr fixed average for the start year and is locked until a refi occurs
  • Rate rule: selects the N cheapest years to refi from historical data in the chosen period (N = refi count, excl. purchase year); a refi fires only if the new rate is ≥1% below the current rate and at least 2 years have passed since the last refi or purchase
  • Balance (rate-and-term): refi to same remaining balance at the new lower rate
  • LTV (cash-out): new loan = max(remaining balance, 75% of current property value). Difference credited to investor cash position
  • Each refi resets to a new 30-yr amortization at the new rate; no closing costs modeled
  • Source: FRED MORTGAGE30US annual averages
  • Rental + refi (tax interaction): rental mortgage interest is tax-deductible. When interest + depreciation exceed rent income, you have a “paper loss” — you owe no tax and may receive a refund, even though you are depositing rent checks. Refinancing to a lower rate reduces that interest deduction, shrinking the paper loss and its tax refund — partially offsetting the payment savings. In some cases the paper loss flips to taxable profit, adding a tax bill. Net refi benefit for rentals is always lower than the face-value payment savings suggest.
  • Primary + refi: standard deduction assumed — no tax complication. Benefit lands in two places: (1) lower monthly PI reduces cumulative costs, improving the cash-flow component of wealth; (2) however, each refi resets to a new 30-yr amortization, so equity builds more slowly and the loan payoff date extends. Within a typical 30-yr window the equity loss often offsets the payment savings — full net benefit usually requires holding 10+ years past the refi date.

Capital Gains: Long-term rate (federal 20% + NIIT 3.8% + state). Stocks/index: gain = proceeds minus initial investment (cost basis). RE rental: 25% depreciation recapture on prior deductions; LT rate on remaining appreciation. RE Primary: §121 excludes $500K (married) / $250K (single) if owned ≥2 years. 1031 defers all tax on rollover.

    States covered by this calculator ▸
    • California (CA) — Prop 13 property tax (1.25% of purchase price, +2%/yr cap). Combined tax rate 44% (35% fed + 9.3% state). Improvement ratio ~40%. High home price-to-rent ratios make all-cash RE a long-term underperformer vs S&P 500; leveraged scenarios are competitive due to appreciation and depreciation shield.
    • Texas (TX) — Market-rate property tax ~2.1%/yr of assessed value. No state income tax (combined rate 35% fed). Higher rent yields than CA; improvement ratio ~70% (more land-efficient builds). Strong job growth drives home price appreciation.
    • Florida (FL) — Homestead exemption applies to primary residences; rental properties taxed at ~1.1%/yr. No state income tax (35% fed). Coastal demand and migration from high-tax states support appreciation. Improvement ratio ~65%.
    • Washington (WA) — Property tax ~1.0%/yr. No state income tax (35% fed). Tech-driven demand in Seattle metro supports above-average home appreciation. Improvement ratio ~50%.
    • New York (NY) — Property tax ~1.7%/yr. Combined tax rate 50% (35% fed + 10.9% state + 3.9% NYC). Dense urban market with high price-to-rent ratios; improvement ratio ~35% (land premium). Depreciation tax shield is most valuable here due to high marginal rate.
    • National average — Blended property tax ~1.1%/yr. Combined rate 38% (35% fed + 3% avg state). FHFA national home price index used for appreciation. Improvement ratio ~55%.
